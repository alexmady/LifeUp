Discover unhelpful thoughts
DISARM: Discover, Identify, Separate, Allow, Recover, Master
Practise 'Thought Monitoring' by using the DISARM technique, which is highly effective in interrupting repetitive unconscious thought patterns and emotions, helping to depersonalise and dissipate them.
D is for Discover unhelpful thoughts
Education
STEP 1: Observe your thoughts
Are you having any involuntary, automatic or repetitive thoughts? Is one thought fuelling another, and so on (snowballing)? Are you judging or labelling, criticising, complaining or overly comparing?
Just observe these thoughts – but don’t judge them.
STEP 2: Scan your body
Direct your attention to the location of the feeling:
  1. Are you feeling any tension, anxiety or stress?
  2. Is there any physical discomfort or unusual sensations?
  3. Are you feeling negativity, irritation, resentment, anger, dissatisfaction?
Consider the sensation and emotion. What situation/event is this linked to? What is the related thought and situation causing this emotion?
Activity
“Thought Snap”
A thought snap is a simple way to take a snapshot of your mind’s thought chatter.
Either in your phone notes or in a journal, write down the following in as few words as possible.
  1. 1. The dominant thought
  2. 2. The situation or likely trigger
  3. 3. Your dominant emotion
  4. 4. Your struggle Rating
The struggle rating is the strength of your emotions and how they are impacting on your state of mind on a scale of 1-10 (where 1 is ‘barely at all’ and 10 is ‘overwhelmed’).
Don’t judge anything as good or bad, don't think too much about it, just notice a thought or emotion and ‘snap’ it. Remember, often it’s the emotion you discover first, which helps you uncover the related situation, from which you then discover the unhelpful thought.
Examples:
'Why doesn't my friend like me?!'
Dave/Davina didn't call me back
Annoyed / insecure
7 out of 10 struggle
Or
'Everyone is relying on me, I can't fail'
Delivery of management report to impossible timeline
Overwhelmed, stressed, burned out, loss of control
8 out of 10 struggle
Or
Trevor is so useless, I can't believe he got promoted over me!
I got passed up for promotion again
Frustrated/resentful/vengeful
5 out of 10 struggle
Take away
'Thought Snap’ your thought chatter to discover any unhelpful thoughts and emotions - an essential part of interrupting, depersonalising and dissipating unhelpful thinking!
Identify the underlying script
DISARM: Discover, Identify, Separate, Allow, Recover, Master
I is for Identify the underlying script
Education
Our internal narrative (what we’ve called ‘thought chatter') sometimes tells us things that aren’t the whole truth. For example we may say to ourselves, ‘I’m superior' or ‘I can't do it'.
Unless we consciously choose to do otherwise, these thoughts will be taken personally and the associated beliefs, feelings and emotions will become integrated into our identity.
Over time this mental conditioning (which is often unhelpful or negative) can feel like it’s part of who we are, something through which we experience the world. This is also the identity through which others experience us, and is often far removed from who we really are or who we want to be.
This identity can be thought of as a mental projection of ourselves or 'self-image'. It acts as a barrier to living in the present moment and can obscure the innate sense of aliveness within us.
No matter how wonderful your upbringing we all carry ‘emotional baggage’ that influences how you think and behave.
The most common core beliefs distil down to either ‘I’m inferior’ or ‘I am superior'. Having these beliefs can lead us into using external means (e.g. relationships, possessions, status) to fill an internal sense of lack or incompleteness.
These unconscious ‘core beliefs’ form the basis of scripts that can run our lives and act as a trigger for negative or unhelpful thoughts and behaviours.
By identifying the script we can further depersonalise and dissipate unhelpful thought patterns.
Activity
Remind yourself of your thought snap from yesterday. Do the dominant thoughts and emotions you identified have an underlying script? If so, it could be one of the following. Do any of these ring true?
  1. I need to have control over the situation, myself, others.
  2. I don’t have the capacity.
  3. This is how the world is.
  4. I’m not allowed to be capable.
  5. I need to be perfect/liked.
  6. I don’t belong.
  7. I’m insignificant / unlovable / not good enough.
  8. Other?
Well done on whatever you discovered, now set your intention for tomorrow!
Take away
By identifying the underlying script driving your dominant thoughts, we further depersonalise and dissipate unhelpful thinking.
Separate yourself from your thoughts (the breakup!)
DISARM: Discover, Identify, Separate, Allow, Recover, Master
S is for Separate yourself from your thoughts
Education
The aim of separation is not to get rid of unpleasant thoughts, but rather to see them for what they are – just unspoken words, and not reality.
You’re not trying to deny your thoughts, push them away or drown them out with positivity. Separation techniques use language, which is closely coupled with thought, to reposition the thought and to show you that the thought is separate from you.
With practice you can expect to separate from your thoughts very quickly, without the need for specific techniques.
Activity
Use your thought snap from the previous day and try out any one of these separation techniques.
Noticing. If you have a thought that upsets or is just unhelpful, state internally, ‘I notice I’m having the thought that…’ This creates distance between you and the unhelpful thoughts.
Naming. Identify your mind’s favourite stories, then give them names. Recognise the common script that plays on repeat. For example, you could say to yourself, ‘That’s just the “I'm unattractive” story again.’ Or you might say, ‘Not that unhelpful thought again, am I really still giving it my attention?’
TV channel. Imagine your thought being aired on a television - simply leave it to play in the background, whilst you focus on something more important.
Journaling. Write down your thoughts, This helps to quiet the mind as the thoughts have an outlet and can be noticed and consciously expressed.
Take away
By using separation techniques, unhelpful thoughts decrease in validity and you are less likely to consider them absolute truth or an accurate representation of reality.
Allowing thoughts and emotions
DISARM: Discover, Identify, Separate, Allow, Recover, Master
A is for Allow any thoughts and emotions
Education
We should not struggle to eliminate unhelpful thoughts. At times they will go away quickly, and at times they won’t. If you start expecting them to go, you are setting yourself up for disappointment or frustration as you battle against them.
You may or may not be able to fully accept a situation but you can at least accept that you are having unhelpful thoughts and emotions about the situation.
Don't try to change or fight them. By allowing them just to be, we do not further fuel them. If we try to fight and resist them they consume our attention and will only grow in strength.
By positively asserting our acceptance of unhelpful thoughts we help ourselves to actually allow them to be.
This approach may seem a little over-engineered at first but remember thoughts drive behaviour and behaviour drives thoughts.
By positively asserting that we allow the unhelpful thoughts 'to be' it drives a behaviour of acceptance and actually further depersonalises and dissipates unhelpful thoughts as they are not fuelled by our attention.
Activity
Use your thought snap from the previous day (or take another) and try out any one of these linguistic allowance techniques.
  1. ‘I accept I am having unhelpful thoughts and emotions about...'
  2. Or just label it 'thinking...'
  3. Thanking your mind. State (internally) 'Thanks, mind!' or 'Thanks, mind for your amazing storytelling ability!' Say it with warmth and humour and with a genuine appreciation for the mind. Just recognise it’s only a story!
Take away
By positively asserting that we allow the unhelpful thoughts 'to be' it drives a behaviour of acceptance and actually further depersonalises and dissipates unhelpful thoughts as they are not fuelled by our attention.
Recover your mind
DISARM: Discover, Identify, Separate, Allow, Recover, Master
R is for Recover your mind
Education
Over thinking and over analysing situations can strain our mental muscle. This can lead to tension headaches and stress, decreasing our effectiveness. This strain also builds up over time and our energy reserves can be depleted, potentially leading to burnout.
To avoid this, take a regular mental break every 90 to 120 minutes. Step away from whatever is driving your thoughts such as your inbox or your life situation. Not sure how to do this? Read on!
Activity
"Thought Break"
A Thought Break is five power breaths.
As always, remember to BREATHE!
  • Breathe Rhythmically, Evenly And, Through the Heart Everytime
To avoid any feelings of discomfort or shortness of breath use ‘ujjayi' (yogic) breathing during your Thought Break. With your mouth closed, inhale and exhale through your nose while gently constricting your throat muscles. If you are doing this correctly, you might sound a bit like you’re very gently snoring!
Ensure a proper exhale to give your lungs a chance to empty. Don’t force the inhale, it will happen naturally if you fully exhale. Allow this slow constricted breathing to rebalance the gases in your bloodstream and relax your mind and body.
Now take your five power breaths as follows:
  1. As you breathe in, count in your mind ‘5 in’, and as you breathe out, count ‘5 out”. Breathe in, count ‘4 in’. Breathe out, count ‘4 out’, and so on.
  2. Continue the count down to ‘1 out’. If you forget what number you are at or are unsure, restart at 5.
  3. The best way to notice if your mind has wandered is to listen to the way you say each number. Listen to the inflection of the count... do you say ‘3 in!!’ or is it more of a ‘3 in.....?’
Take away
Constant overthinking can strain our mental muscle. So take a regular ‘thought break’.
Mastery of your thoughts
DISARM: Discover, Identify, Separate, Allow, Recover, Master
M is for Mastery over your thoughts
Education
When your attention wanders onto your unhelpful thoughts they can snowball and grow in your mind until they cloud your views and behaviour.
Picture your mind like a clear blue sky, expansive and ever-present. Now imagine your thoughts floating by like clouds. Remember there is a calm, still peaceful place behind turbulent thoughts and feelings.
The sky can always make room for the weather. The calm follows the storm. The calm sky is always there when the weather has passed, still and expansive.
It’s easy to forget that you have a choice whether to believe your thoughts or not, a choice whether to take them personally; a choice to find the space around thoughts.
By regularly noticing we are not our thoughts and by making a regular positive assertion that we are separate from our thoughts, we re-gain mastery of our minds.
Activity
Read this sentence a few times, silently to yourself.
‘I can hear the voice in my head reading this sentence’
Notice how you can listen to the soundtrack of thoughts inside your head? This is a little trickier the first time you try it so don't worry if it wasn't obvious. With a little practice you can notice how you can observe your own thought process objectively.
Now say this sentence a few times, silently to yourself. Listen as you say it.
I choose not to personalise my thoughts’ about... [insert thought here]
Take away
By regularly observing that we are not our thoughts, we re-gain mastery of our minds.
